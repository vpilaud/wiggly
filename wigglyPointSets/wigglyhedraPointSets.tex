\section{Wiggly complex}
\label{sec:wigglyComplex}

Throughout this paper, we consider an arbitrary point set~$P$ in the plane~$\R^2$ (neither necessarily aligned, nor necessarily in general position).
A point~$p \in P$ is \defn{extremal} if it is a vertex of the convex hull of~$P$.
We denote by~$n = v + w$ the number of points of~$P$, where~$v$ are extremal and~$w$ are not.
We first define the wiggly segments of~$P$.
Intuitively, a wiggly segment is like a segment~$[p,q]$ between two points~$p, q \in P$, except that the path locally deviates to one side or the other of every point in the open interval~$]p,q[$.

\begin{definition}
\label{def:wigglySegments}
An \defn{oriented arc} from~$p$ to~$q$ is an isotopy class in the set of continuous maps~$\alpha : [0,1] \mapsto \R^2$ with~$\alpha(0) = p$, $\alpha(1) = q$ and~$\alpha(]0,1[) \subseteq \R^2 \ssm P$.
%We say that~$p$ and~$q$ are the \defn{endpoints} of~$\arc$, and that $\arc$ is \defn{incident} to $p$ and~$q$.
An \defn{arc} is an oriented arc modulo reorientation.
An arc is \defn{internal} if its interior intersects the convex hull of~$P$, and \defn{external} otherwise.
The \defn{length} of an arc is the infimum of the lengths of the representatives in its isotopy class.
A \defn{wiggly segment} is an arc whose length is the Euclidean distance between its endpoints.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}
\label{def:compatible}
A set~$D$ of wiggly segments is
\begin{itemize}
\item \defn{non-crossing} if there exists a set of representatives of the arcs of~$D$ with pairwise disjoint relative interiors,
\item \defn{pointed} if for any point~$p \in P$, the set of points~$q \in Q$ so that~$D$ contains a wiggly segment with endpoints~$\{p,q\}$ is contained in an open half-space at~$p$,
\item \defn{compatible} if it is both non-crossing and pointed.
\end{itemize}
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}
\label{def:wigglyDissection}
A \defn{wiggly dissection} of~$P$ is a compatible set~$D$ of wiggly segments of~$P$ containing all external wiggly segments.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}
\label{def:wigglyComplex}
The \defn{wiggly complex}~$\reducedWigglyComplex_P$ is the simplicial complex of compatible subsets of internal wiggly segments on~$P$.

\begin{remark}
A few observations about \cref{def:wigglySegments,def:compatible,def:wigglyDissection,def:wigglyComplex}:
\begin{enumerate}
\item The wiggly segments could also be encoded by quadruples~$(p, q, L, R)$ with~$p,q \in P$ and~$L \sqcup R  = {]p,q[} \cap P$, modulo reorientation~$(p, q, L, R) \sim (q, p, R, L)$, and the non-crossing and pointedness conditions are then purely combinatorial (see~\cite{BapatPilaud}). In this paper, we find it more intuitive and convenient to define and visualize wiggly arcs and the wiggly complex in terms of isotopy classes of arcs.
\item As the $v$ external wiggly segments are compatible with any other wiggly segments, it is convenient to ignore them in the definition of the wiggly complex~$\reducedWigglyComplex_P$, but to include them in all wiggly dissections.
In other words, the \defn{extended wiggly complex}~$\extendedWigglyComplex_P$, defined as the simplicial complex of compatible subsets of all wiggly segments on~$P$, is a $v$-fold cone over the wiggly complex~$\reducedWigglyComplex_P$.
\item \cref{def:compatible,def:wigglyDissection,def:wigglyComplex} were already largely studied in two specific situations. Namely, we refer to~\cite{BapatPilaud} for the case of aligned points, and to the original article of~\cite{PocchiolaVegter} and to the survey~\cite{RoteSantosStreinu-pseudotriangulations} for the case of points in general position.
\end{enumerate}
\end{remark}

The goal of this paper is to prove the following statement, already proved for aligned points in~\cite{BapatPilaud} and for points in general position in~\cite{RoteSantosStreinu-polytope}.

\begin{theorem}
\label{thm:polytope}
The wiggly complex~$\reducedWigglyComplex_P$ is isomorphic to the boundary complex of a simple polytope of dimension~$v+2w-3$.
\end{theorem}

\cref{thm:polytope} implies in particular that~$\reducedWigglyComplex_P$ is a simplicial sphere of dimension~$v+2w-4$, a result independently established in~\cite[Coro.~3.21]{BapatDeopurkarLicata}.
Note that their proof proceeds via a deformation argument, describing how the wiggly complex varies under a small perturbation of the point set.
By contrast, our approach gives a direct polytopal realization, but it depends on the fact that the wiggly complex~$\reducedWigglyComplex_P$ is a simplicial pseudomanifold without boundary of dimension~$v+2w-4$.
We thus  sketch a direct proof of this fact, both to avoid reliance on~\cite[Coro.~3.21]{BapatDeopurkarLicata} and because we believe it offers useful geometric and combinatorial insight into the structure of the wiggly complex.

\begin{definition}
Consider a wiggly dissection~$D$ of~$P$ (we actually implicitly consider a set of pairwise non-crossing representatives of the arcs of~$D$).
The arcs of~$D$ separate the plane~$\R^2$ into connected components, whose closure we call \defn{wiggly cells}.
We call \defn{external} cell the one outside the external wiggly arcs, and \defn{internal} all other cells.
Note that each internal wiggly cell~$C$ has an external boundary component (separating it from the external cell), and may have some internal boundary component.
The \defn{corners} of~$C$ are the convex vertices along its boundary and the \defn{chains} of~$C$ are the pieces of its boundary between two consecutive corners.
A \defn{wiggly triangle} is a wiggly cell with $3$ corners and no internal boundary.
A \defn{wiggly quadrangle} is a wiggly cell with $4$ corners and no internal boundary, or a wiggly digon with a single interior point.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}
Any wiggly dissection~$D$ of a set of $n$ points contains at most $2n-3$ wiggly segments and at most~$n-2$ internal wiggly cells.
Moreover, the following assertions are equivalent:
\begin{enumerate}[(i)]
\item $D$ is an inclusion maximal wiggly dissection,
\item all wiggly cells of~$D$ are wiggly triangles,
\item $D$ has $n-2$ internal wiggly cells and is connected,
\item $D$ has $2n-3$ wiggly segments.
\end{enumerate}
We then say that~$D$ is a \defn{wiggly triangulation}.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
\para{(i) $\Rightarrow$ (ii)}
Consider a wiggly cell~$C$ of a maximal wiggly dissection~$D$.
Note that~$C$ has at least $2$ corners, and at least $3$ if it has no internal boundary.
Consider now an arc~$\arc$ between two corners of~$C$.
Stretch~$\arc$ until you get a geodesic~$\arc'$ inside~$C$, and cut~$\arc'$ into subarcs at the points of~$P$ it contains.
The resulting arcs are wiggly segments compatible with~$D$ (they are non-crossing with~$D$ because~$\arc$ was inside~$C$, and they are pointed with~$D$ because the endpoints of~$\arc$ are corners of~$C$).
By maximality of~$D$, it implies that all these wiggly segments already belong to~$D$.
In other words, the geodesic~$\arc$ is a chain of~$R$.
This observation implies that~$C$ has precisely $3$ corners (otherwise, we have an arc joining two non consecutive corners of~$R$, thus not isotopic to a chain of~$R$) and no internal boundary component (otherwise there is a loop at a corner of~$R$ not isotopic to a chain of~$R$).
In other words, $C$ is a wiggly triangle.

\para{(ii) $\Rightarrow$ (iii)}
Assume that all cells of a wiggly dissection~$D$ are wiggly triangles.
Note that this implies in particular that~$D$ is connected (as otherwise some wiggly cell would have an internal connected component).
Moreover, assume that $D$ has $n$ vertices, $m$ edges, $c$ internal cells, and~$a$ angles.
Then $n-e+r=1$ (Euler's relation), $a = 2e$ (because the number of corners around each vertex is its degree) and $a = 3r + n$ (because each cell has at $3$ convex angles, and $D$ has precisely $n$ concave angles, since each vertex is pointed).
We conclude that~$e = 2n-3$ and~$r = n-2$.

\para{(iii) $\Rightarrow$ (iv)}
If~$D$ is connected and has~$n-2$ internal wiggly cells, then~$e = n + r - 1 = 2n - 3$ by Euler's relation.

\para{(iv) $\Rightarrow$ (i)}
Any wiggly dissection~$D$ is contained in an inclusion maximal wiggly dissection~$D'$.
We have shown that (i) $\Rightarrow$ (ii) $\Rightarrow$ (iii) $\Rightarrow$ (iv), so that~$D'$ has~$2n-3$ wiggly segments.
We obtain that $D$ has at most $2n-3$ wiggly segments and at most~$n-2$ wiggly cells.
Moreover, if~$D$ has $2n-3$ edges, the~$D = D'$ is inclusion maximal.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:diagonals}
Any wiggly quadrangle~$Q$ admits precisely two wiggly diagonals, \ie wiggly segments of~$P$ inside~$Q$ and compatible with~$Q$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
If~$Q$ is a wiggly digon with a single interior point, then it contains precisely two arcs which are non crossing and not isotopic to the boundary (namely joining one of the corners to the interior point), and they are both compatible with~$Q$.
Otherwise, the diagonals of~$Q$ are the two wiggly segments in the interior of~$Q$ along the geodesics obtained by stretching the two arcs joining the two pairs of opposite corners of~$Q$.
\end{proof}

\begin{definition}
The \defn{wiggly flip graph}~$\wigglyFlipGraph_P$ of~$P$ is the graph with a vertex for each wiggly triangulation of~$P$, and with an edge between two wiggly triangulations~$T$ and~$T'$ of~$P$ if and only if there are wiggly segments~$\arc \in T$ and~$\arc' \in T'$ such that~$T \ssm \{\arc\} = T' \ssm \{\arc'\}$.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}
The wiggly flip graph~$\wigglyFlipGraph_P$ is regular of degree~$v+2w-3$ and connected.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
Consider an internal wiggly segment~$\arc$ in a wiggly triangulation~$T$ of~$P$.
Let~$Q$ denote the wiggly quadrangle obtained by merging the two wiggly triangles incident to~$\arc$.
By~\cref{prop:diagonals}, there are precisely two wiggly segments of~$P$ inside~$Q$ and compatible with~$Q$.
This proves that~$\wigglyFlipGraph_P$ is $(v+2w-3)$-regular.

Consider now an arbitrary order~$p_1, \dots, p_n$ of~$P$ such that~$p_i$ lies on the complement of the convex hull of~$\{p_1, \dots, p_{i-1}\}$ for all~$i \in [n]$.
For all~$i \in [n]$, define inductively the wiggly triangulation~$T_i^\circ$ of~$P_i \eqdef \{p_1, \dots, p_i\}$ obtained from~$T_{i-1}^\circ$ by adding all external wiggly segments of~$P_i$.
We prove by induction on~$i$ that any wiggly triangulation of~$P_i$ is connected by flips to~$T_i^\circ$.
For any wiggly triangulation~$T_i$ of~$P_i$, consider the wiggly triangulation~$T_{i-1}$ obtained from~$T_i$ by flipping all internal wiggly segments incident to~$p_i$, and then deleting~$p_i$ and its two incident external wiggly arcs.
Then~$T_{i-1}$ is a wiggly triangulation of~$P_{i-1}$, which is connected by flips to~$T_{i-1}^\circ$ by induction.
We conclude that~$T_i$ is connected by flip to~$T_i^\circ$.
\end{proof}

Our construction of the wiggly fan is based on rigidity considerations, similar to those used in~\cite{RoteSantosStreinu-polytope} to construct the fan (and the polytope) of pseudo-triangulations of a point set. In fact, for point sets in general position our construction restricts to the one in that paper. Before looking at the case of wiggly arcs, let us briefly recall the basics of (infinitesimal) rigidity theory.

Let $P \subset \R^2$ be a point set , consisting of $n$ points $p_1,\dots,p_n$. The~\defn{infinitesimal rigidity matrix} of $P$ is the following $\binom{n}{2} \times 2n$ matrix:
\[
R(P) \eqdef
\begin{pmatrix}
p_1-p_2 & p_2-p_1 & 0 & \cdots & 0 & 0 \\
p_1-p_3 & 0 & p_3-p_1 & \cdots & 0 & 0 \\
\vdots & \vdots & \vdots & & \vdots & \vdots \\
p_1-p_n & 0 & 0 & \cdots & 0 & p_n-p_1 \\
0 & p_2-p_3 & p_3-p_2 & \cdots & 0 & 0 \\
\vdots & \vdots & \vdots & & \vdots & \vdots \\
0 & 0 & 0 & \cdots & p_{n-1}-p_n & p_n-p_{n-1}
\end{pmatrix}
\]
That is to say, we have a row vector $R(p,q)\in (\R^2)^n \cong (\R^2)^P$ associated to each pair of points~${p,q\in P}$.
All entries in $R(p,q)$ are zero except for two blocks of size two: the block corresponding to $p$ has the vector $p-q$ and the block corresponding to $q$ has the block $q-p$.

The matrix $R(P)$, considered as a linear map $(\R^2)^n\to \R^{\binom{n}{2}}$, has the following interpretation: each~${v = (v_1,\dots,v_n) \in (\R^2)^n}$ is understood as a vector of velocities (or infinitesimal displacements) for the points in $P$, and the vector $R(P) v \in \R^{\binom{n}{2}}$ returns in the coordinate $\{i,j\} \in \R^{\binom{n}{2}}$ the value~$(p_i-p_j)\cdot (v_i-v_j)$, which equals the (infinitesimal) increase or decrease of the length of the segment $[p_i,q_i]$ under that motion.

The kernel of $R(P)$ are the infinitesimal motions that do not change any distance between points, that is, moving the whole point set as if it was a rigid block. If $P$ affinely spans $\R^2$ (which we always assume) then $R(P)$ has rank $2n-3$ and its three-dimensional kernel is generated by the vectors of the form $T_v:=(v,...,v)$ for an arbitrary $v\in \R^2$ plus the vector 
\[
P^\perp:=(p_1^\perp, \dots, p_n^\perp),
\]
where $(x,y)^\perp:=(-y,x)$ denotes the rotation of~$(x,y)$ by \(90^{\circ}\) in the positive direction.
The former represent \defn{global translations} and the latter an (infinitesimal) \defn{rotation} with center at the origin.
We denote\footnote{Although we only need to think of it as a linear space, our reason for the notation $se(P)$ is that this kernel equals the Lie algebra of the Lie group $SE(2)$ of Euclidean motions of the plane, acting as global rigid motions on $P$. Observe that the kernel contains also the rotations with centers at points other than the origin, since the rotation with center at $o\in \R^2$ produces as infinitesimal motion of $P$ the vector $P^\perp - T_o$.} this kernel $se(P)$.

The dual map $R(P)^T  : \R^{\binom{n}{2}} \to (\R^2)^n$ also has a physical interpretation. We can think of each pair of points in $P$ as joined by a rod, and interpret each vector $w\in \R^{\binom{n}{2}}$ as expressing extension or compression stresses applied to the individual rods. Then the vector $R(P)^T w  \in (\R^2)^n$  equals $(f_1,\dots,f_n)$ where $f_i = \sum_j w_{ij} (p_i-p_j)$ is the resulting force at point $p_i$. The kernel of $R(P)^T$ hence represents the stresses that can be applied to the rods and produce no net force on each $p_i$. These are called \defn{equilibrium stresses}.

If we now consider a set of edges $E \subseteq \binom{n}{2}$, this defines a restricted matrix $R_E(P)$ whose kernel are the infinitesimal motions preserving the lengths of edges in $E$. Hence, $E$ is called (infinitesimally) \defn{rigid} (for the positions $P$) if $R_E(P)$ has full-rank, equal to $2n-3$. Similarly, the kernel of $R_E(P)^T$ represents the equilibrium stresses of the subgraph $E$, and $E$ is called \defn{stress-free} if that kernel is zero; that is, if $R_E(P)$ has rank $E$.%
\footnote{This information is captured by the linear matroid of rows of $R(P)$: rigid graphs are the spanning sets of rows, and stress-free graphs are the independent ones). Consequently, that matroid is called the (infinitisimal) rigidity matroid of $P$.}

The main result of  \cite{RoteSantosStreinu-polytope} is that when $P$ is in general position (and hence,  wiggly arcs are just straightline segments), the wiggly fan can be embedded associating to each vertex $\{i,j\}$ the corresponding rigidity vector $R(i,j)$, producing a complete polytopal fan. In order to generalize this to non-general position we need to modify the definition of rigidity vector associated to a wiggly arc.

Let $\arc=(p, q, L, R)$ be a wiggly arc, represented by its end-points $p$ and $q$ and the set $L\cup R$ of points of $P$ in $]p,q[ \cap P$, divided in those lying to the right and to the left of $\arc$, when going from $p$ to $q$.

We introduce the following notation: for each $r\in P$ let $\mathbbm{1}_r$ denote the matrix in $(\R^{2\times 2})^P \cong \R^{2\times 2n}$ having an identity $2\times 2$ matrix in the block of $r$ and zeroes elsewhere. Observe that for an $x\in \R^2$ we have that $x \mathbbm{1}_r \in (\R^2)^P$ equals the vector  that has $x$ in the $r$th coordinate and zeroes elsewhere. 
Hence, the usual rigidity vector $R(p,q)$ as defined above can be expressed as follows:

\begin{definition}\label{def:lrv}
  The~\defn{longitudinal rigidity vector} \(R_l(\arc)\) of \(\arc\) is defined as
  \[
  R_l(\arc) := (p-q) (\mathbbm{1}_p  - \mathbbm{1}_q).
  \]
\end{definition}

To this vector we add a transversal part depending on the intermediate points along the segment $[p,q]$.
Here, a vector in $(\R^2)^P$ is called \defn{transversal} to the segment $[p,q]$ if it is of the form
$(q-p)^\perp \left(\sum_{r\in P} \lambda_r \mathbbm{1}_r\right)$ with $\lambda_r = 0$ if $r\not\in [p,q]$.

For each point $r\in L\cup R$, let $\lambda_r$ and $\mu_r$ denote the barycentric coordinates of $r$ in $[p,q]$. That is, $\lambda,\mu\in [0,1]$ are the unique numbers such that
\[
r = \lambda_r p + \mu_r  q,
\quad
\lambda_r  + \mu_r  = 1
\]
or, equivalently, 
\[
r-p = \mu_r  (q-p), 
\quad
q-r = \lambda_r  (q-p).
\]

\begin{definition}\label{def:trv}
For each $r\in P \cap\, ]p,q[$ consider the following vector, transversal to $[p,q]$:
\begin{align*}
R_t(p,q;r) \eqdef& 
(q-p)^\perp \mathbbm{1}_r - (q-r)^\perp  \mathbbm{1}_p - (r-p)^\perp  \mathbbm{1}_q) \\
=&(q-p)^\perp (\mathbbm{1}_r - \lambda_r \mathbbm{1}_p - \mu_r \mathbbm{1}_q ).
\end{align*}
  The~\defn{transversal rigidity vector} \(R_t(\arc)\) of $\arc=(p, q, L, R)$ is a row vector in \((\R^2)^P\) defined as 
  \[
  R_t(\arc) \eqdef \sum_{r\in L} R_t(p,q;r) - \sum_{r\in R} R_t(p,q;r).
  \]
\end{definition}

since

The normal fan of the polytope mentioned in~\cref{thm:polytope} is spanned by rigidity vectors of wiggly edges, which we now define.

All rigidity vectors lie in \((\R^2)^P \cong (\R^2)^n\).
The rigidity vector of \(\arc\) is the combination of a longitudinal component whose every coordinate is parallel to \(\arc\), and a transversal component whose every coordinate is orthogonal to \(\arc\).
The longitudinal component is zero at any coordinate that is not an endpoint.
The transversal component is zero at any coordinate that is neither an endpoint nor a waypoint.
The longitudinal rigidity vector was used extensively in~\cite{RoteSantosStreinu-polytope} to construct a polytope in the case of points in general position.
When \(P\) is arbitrary, we need to perturb the longitudinal rigidity vector by a small multiple of the transversal rigidity vector.
We show in ??? that this procedure gives us a complete normal fan.

Given \(x \in \R^2\) and \(r \in P\), denote by \(x \mathbbm{1}_r\) or \(x \cdot \mathbbm{1}_r\) the vector in \((\R^2)^P\) that has \(x\) in the \(r\)th coordinate and zero elsewhere.

We now assemble the rigidity vectors into rigidity matrices.
All of these have rows indexed by the wiggly arcs on \(P\).
Each row is a rigidity vector, and thus lies in \((\R^2)^P\).
\begin{definition}\label{def:rm}
  The~\defn{longitudinal rigidity matrix} of \(P\) is the matrix \(M_l(P)\) whose row indexed by \(\arc\) is \(R_l(\arc)\).
The~\defn{transverse rigidity matrix} of \(P\) is the matrix \(M_t(P)\) whose row indexed by \(\arc\) is \(R_t(\arc)\).
The~\defn{(total) \(\epsilon\)-rigidity matrix} of \(P\) is the matrix \(M^{\epsilon}(P)\) defined as \(M_l(P) + \epsilon M_t(P)\).
\end{definition}
To justify the name~\emph{rigidity matrix}, we need to consider infinitesimal motions of point sets.
An~\defn{infinitesimal motion} of the point set \(P\) is the assignment of a vector \(v_p \in \R^2\) for each point \(p \in P\).
These can be assembled into an infinitesimal motion vector \((v_p)_{p \in P} \in (\R^2)^P\).
We think of such a vector as the tangent vector at \(P\) to a differentiable path in the configuration space of point sets.
The rigid motions of the entire plane \(\R^2\) are translations and rotations, and their tangent vectors at \(P\) are the infinitesimal translations and infinitesimal rotations, respectively.
Precisely, an~\defn{infinitesimal translation} assigns a constant \(v \in \R^2\) to each \(p \in P\).
An~\defn{infinitesimal rotation} around some point \(w \in \R^2\) assigns a fixed scalar multiple of \((p - w)^{\perp}\) to each \(p \in P\).
The vector space spanned by all infinitesimal translations and rotations is called the space of~\defn{infinitesimal rigid motions}.
It is easy to check that this is a 3-dimensional subspace of \((\R^2)^P\).
\begin{proposition}
  For any \(\epsilon > 0\), the \(\epsilon\)-rigidity matrix \(M^{\epsilon}(P)\) has rank \(2n - 3\).
  Its kernel is precisely the space of infinitesimal rigid motions.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
  It is easy to check that the infinitesimal rigid motions all lie in the kernel of \(M^{\epsilon}(P)\).
  Let us show that nothing else lies in the kernel.
  Let \(\b{v} = (v_p)_{p \in P}\) be an infinitesimal motion that lies in the kernel of \(M^{\epsilon}(P)\).
  By possibly adding a rigid motion to \(b(v)\), we may assume that the first two points of \(P\) are fixed by \(\b(v)\).
  That is, \(v_{p_1} = v_{p_2} = 0\).
  Suppose for contradiction that there is some point \(p_k \in P\) such that \(v_{p_k} \neq 0\).

  First suppose that \(p_k\) does not lie on the line \(p_1p_2\), which means that \(p_1\), \(p_2\), and \(p_k\) form a triangle.
  By moving \(p_k\) closer to \(p_1\) if necessary, assume that \(v_{p_i} = 0\) for all intermediate points \(p_i \in P\) that lie on the open segment \(]p_1,p_k[\).
  Similarly by moving \(p_{k}\) closer to \(p_2\) if necessary, assume that \(v_{p_i} = 0\) for all intermediate points \(p_i \in P\) on the open segment \(]p_2,p_k[\).

  Only finitely many points of \(P\) lie in the original triangle \(p_1p_2p_k\).
  Thus by iterating the above process a finite number of times, we may assume that for each \(p_i \in P\) such that \(p_i \in ]p_1,p_k[\) or \(p_i \in ]p_2,p_k[\), we have \(v_{p_i} = 0\).
  
  Let \(\arc\) be any wiggly segment with endpoints \(p_1\) and \(p_k\).
  Recall that \(M^{\epsilon}(P) \cdot \b{v} = 0\), and consider the entry indexed by \(\arc\) of this product.
  The \(\arc\)th row of \(M^{\epsilon}(P)\) is non-zero only at the endpoints and waypoints of \(\arc\).
  Among these coordinates, \(\b{v}\) is non-zero only at \(p_k\).
  Thus by~\cref{def:rv,def:lrv,def:trv,def:rm}, we obtain the equation
  \[(p_k - p_1)\cdot v_{p_k} = 0.\]
  Thus \(v_{p_k}\) is orthogonal to \(p_k - p_1\).
  Repeating this argument for \(p_2\), we see that \(v_{p_k}\) is orthogonal to \(p_k - p_2\) as well.
  Since \(p_k\) does not lie on the line \(p_1p_2\), it follows that \(v_{p_k} = 0\), giving the desired contradiction.

  Now suppose that \(p_k\) lies on the line \(p_1p_2\).
  By inductively computing the entries of \(M^{\epsilon}(P) \cdot \b(v)\) corresponding to segments between successive points on this line, we can check that \(v_{p_k}\) is orthogonal to The line \(p_1p_2\).

  If \(p_k\in ]p_1,p_2[\), let \(\arc\) be a wiggly segment from \(p_1\) to \(p_2\)
  such that in a neighbourhood of any waypoint \(p_i\), the arc \(\arc\) and the vector \(v_{p_i}\) lie on opposite sides of the line \(p_1p_2\).
  Then the only contribution to the \(\arc\)th entry of \(M^{\epsilon}(P) \cdot \b{v}\) is from the transversal rigidity vectors, and the output is strictly positive.
  This is a contradiction.

  Suppose \(p_1\) lies between \(p_k\) and \(p_2\) on the line \(p_1p_2\).
  By changing \(p_k\) if necessary, assume that it is the closest point in \(P\) to \(p_1\) on this line such that \(v(p_k) \ne 0\).
  By the previous argument, every point \(p_i \in [p_1,p_2]\) has \(v_{p_i} = 0\).
  Consider any wiggly segment \(\arc\) with endpoints \(p_k\) and \(p_2\).
  Then the only contribution to \(M^{\epsilon}(P)\cdot \b{v}\) comes from the transverse rigidity vectors, and it is a single dot product of \(v_k\) with a non-zero parallel vector.
  This is non-zero unless \(v_{p_k} = 0\), and we again get a contradiction.

  We conclude that \(v_{p_k} = 0\) for all \(p_k \in P\), proving that the kernel of \(M^{\epsilon}(P)\) is spanned by the infinitesimal rigid motions.
\end{proof}

\section{Proof}
\label{sec:proof}

%\begin{definition}
%Let $\b{R} : V \to \R^d$ be a vector configuration and~$f : V \to \R$ be a height function with the same label set~$V$.
%The \defn{coherent subdivision} of~$(\b{R}, f)$ is the projection into~$\R^V$ of the lower hull of the 
%\end{definition}

The proof relies on the following statement.

\begin{theorem}
Let~$\simplex$ be a connected $(d-1)$-dimensional pseudomanifold without boundary, and let~$\b{R} : V \to \R^d$ be a vector configuration and~$f : V \to \R^+$ be a height function both labeled by the vertex set~$V$ of~$\simplex$.
Then the following are equivalent:
\begin{itemize}
  %\item $\simplex$ is the coherent triangulation of the vector configuration~$\b{R}$ with respect to the height function~$f$,
  \item $\simplex$ is the boundary complex of the convex hull of the points~$f(v) \, \b{R}(v)$ for all~$v \in V$,
  \item For every minimal non-face~$F$ of~$\simplex$, there is a linear dependence~$\lambda: V \to \R$ of the vector configuration~$\b{R}$ whose negative part is~$F$ and which evaluates negatively with~$f$, that is
  \[
    F = \set{v \in V}{\lambda(v) < 0},
    \qquad
    \sum_{v \in V} \lambda(v) \, \b{R}(v) = \b{0},
    \qquad\text{and}\qquad
    \sum_{v \in V} \lambda(v) \, f(v) < 0.
  \]
\end{itemize}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
\paco{revisar que est\'a claro cuando se consideran $T$ y $\simplex$ como ``complejos simpliciales abstractos'' y cuando como abanico y complejo simplicial geométrico, respectivamente}
  Let $T$ be the regular triangulation of $\b{R}(V)$ given by $f$). 
  That is, $T$ is a simplicial complex which embedded geometrically on $\b{R}(V)$ forms a simplicial fan covering $\cone(\b{R}(V))$.
  
  Assume $\simplex=T$; that is, $\Delta$ is the boundary complex of the convex hull of the points~$f(v) \, \b{R}(v)$ for all~$v\in V$. Consider $F$, a minimal non-face of $\simplex$.
  
  Choose a point $x\in \relint{\b{R}(F)}$.
  Since $T$ is a triangulation covering $\cone{\b{R}(V)}$, there is a unique $G\in T$ such that $x\in \relint{G}$.
  As $x\in \conv{F} \cap\cone{G}$, there are two convex combinations
  \[
    x = \sum_{p_i\in G} \lambda_i p_i = \sum_{q_j\in F} \mu_j q_j,
  \]
  with all $\lambda_i, \mu_j \geq 0$, unique up to scaling.
  Furthermore, no $\mu_j$ can be $0$ since $F$ is a minimal non-face of $\simplex$, and no
  $\lambda_i$ can be $0$ because $G$ is the minimal face of $T$ containing $x$.
  
  Hence, the linear dependence $\sum_{p_i \in G} \lambda_i p_i - \sum_{q_j\in F} \mu_j q_j$ has
  $F$ as its negative part.
  Finally, since $F$ is a non-face of $\simplex$, its relative interior is contained in the interior of $\conv(\b{R}(V))$, so
\paco{Why are there no cancellations in this formula?}
  \[
    \sum_{p_i \in G} \lambda_i f(p_i) - \sum_{q_j\in F} \mu_j f(q_j) < 0.
  \]
  
  For the converse, assume this is true for all minimal non-faces.
  For each minimal non-face $F$, consider the positive support of its linear dependence,
  $G = \lambda^+$.
  While $G$ is not necessarily a face of $T$, it is below $F$, so $F$ is not a face of $T$.
  
  As this is true for all minimal non-faces, we have that $T \subseteq \simplex$.
  Now, two connected pseudo-manifolds (without bounddary) of the same dimension vccannot be properly contained in one another, so  $T = \simplex$.

\end{proof}

The first step of the proof is thus to understand the minimal non faces of the wiggly complex~$\reducedWigglyComplex_P$.
